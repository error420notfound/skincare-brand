There is a tendency in contemporary skincare to treat fermentation as a trend—another item in the long list of innovations that arrive with breathless copy and depart without ceremony. We would like to argue that this framing misrepresents what fermentation actually is, and what it can do for the skin.

Fermentation is not new. It predates recorded history. The transformation of raw materials through microbial activity has been deployed by every human culture for food, medicine, and ritual. What is new is our ability to control it precisely, to direct specific strains of microorganisms toward specific outcomes, and to verify those outcomes through rigorous assay.

## What Fermentation Does to an Ingredient

When we ferment Kakadu plum for the Lumière Renewing Serum, we are not simply preserving the fruit or enhancing its stability. We are fundamentally changing its molecular composition.

The vitamin C present in raw Kakadu plum (ascorbic acid) is highly bioactive but also highly unstable—it oxidises on contact with air and degrades rapidly in aqueous formulations. Through 48-hour cold fermentation with a precisely controlled *Lactobacillus* strain, we convert a portion of the ascorbic acid to more stable derivatives—sodium ascorbyl phosphate and ascorbyl glucoside—while simultaneously producing a range of organic acids that lower the pH of the formula, improving its penetration into the epidermis.

The fermented extract is more stable, more bioavailable, and more consistently efficacious than the raw equivalent. We have measured this.

## The Barrier Question

There is a secondary effect of fermentation that we find equally compelling: its impact on the skin's microbiome.

The skin hosts approximately one trillion microorganisms—bacteria, fungi, and viruses that form a complex ecosystem in dynamic equilibrium. This ecosystem is not incidental; it is structural. The microbiome contributes to barrier function, regulates pH, and modulates immune response. When it is disrupted—by harsh surfactants, by overuse of active ingredients, by environmental stress—the consequences manifest as sensitivity, redness, and compromised defence against pathogens.

Fermented ingredients contain postbiotics: the metabolic byproducts of microbial activity that have been shown to support the proliferation of beneficial skin bacteria while inhibiting the growth of opportunistic strains like *Cutibacterium acnes*.

## Our Process

We ferment in small batches, in temperature-controlled vessels maintained at 22–26°C to optimise for our target organisms without producing unwanted competing fermentation. Each batch is tested for pH, microbial load, and target compound concentration before and after the process.

The fermentation vessels are glass—never plastic, which can leach plasticisers into the medium at the temperatures and pH levels involved. The water used is purified by reverse osmosis and UV treatment. The starter cultures are maintained in our own library, passaged carefully to preserve their genetic stability.

This level of process control is unusual for a cosmetic manufacturer. It is not unusual for a pharmaceutical one. We believe the distinction is increasingly artificial.

## A Final Note

We do not use fermentation as a marketing device. We use it because, in specific applications, it produces demonstrably superior results. Where it does not—where the raw ingredient performs equally well or better—we use the raw ingredient.

The Brume Radiance Tonic uses fermented rice water because the fermentation process is essential to the efficacy of inositol as a skin-conditioning agent. The Ombre Moisture Cream does not use fermented ingredients, because the ceramide complex at its core performs optimally in its conventional chemical form.

We try, in all things, to follow the evidence.
