The cedars of the Atlas mountains have been felled and traded since the time of the Phoenicians. Egyptian burial records mention cedarwood oil. The Epic of Gilgamesh—the oldest surviving work of literature—describes the hero's journey to the Cedar Forest as the most formidable challenge he has undertaken. When we say that cedarwood is the world's oldest luxury aromatic, this is not hyperbole.

We travelled to the forests of the Haut Atlas in late autumn, when the air carried a particular cold clarity and the trees were at their most fragrant. Our purpose was to understand, with the directness that only proximity provides, why this material has endured across four thousand years of human refinement.

## The Tree

*Cedrus atlantica* is not, technically, a true cedar. It is more accurately classified as a member of the pine family—a distinction that matters to botanists and very few others. What matters for our purposes is its wood: dense, resinous, and extraordinarily aromatic due to a high concentration of sesquiterpene hydrocarbons, particularly cedrene and cedrol.

The trees we visited were between 80 and 150 years old. They are not harvested; they are managed. The essential oil is extracted from the wood chips and sawdust produced during legitimate forestry operations—thinning that maintains forest health and allows the older trees to continue growing. We use only oil from operations certified by the Forest Stewardship Council.

## The Extraction

Steam distillation: water is heated to steam beneath a chamber packed with freshly milled wood chips. The aromatic molecules volatilise, travel upward with the steam, condense in a cooling coil, and separate by density in a Florentine flask. The essential oil floats above the hydrosol.

The process takes four to six hours for a single batch. The yield is low—approximately 3% by weight of the raw material. This is part of why the oil commands its price, and part of why its adulteration is common. We test every batch for specific gravity, optical rotation, and refractive index. We can identify adulteration at concentrations below 5%.

## The Smell

There is a particular difficulty in describing cedarwood to someone who has not smelled it. It is warm without sweetness, dry without sharpness. Woody, yes, but with an almost mineral quality—like a stone that has absorbed years of forest light.

In the Encens de Cèdre candle, we use it as the structural backbone of the fragrance. The frankincense provides the resinous foreground, the yuzu the opening brightness, the vetiver the deep finish. But the cedar is what makes it coherent—what makes it smell, as one of our perfumers put it, like somewhere you would want to be.

## A Note on Sustainability

The Atlas cedar forests have declined significantly over the past century, primarily due to overgrazing and climate-driven drought. This is not an abstraction for us—it is a material risk to an ingredient we depend on. We contribute 1% of revenue from cedarwood-containing products to forest restoration programmes in the Haut Atlas through our partnership with the High Atlas Foundation.

We are aware that one percent is not enough. We document this honestly rather than presenting it as resolution.
